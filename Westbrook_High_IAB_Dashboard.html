Performance by Standard

Two writing standards are tested across the 10 items
CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.WHST.11-12.8

Writing Standards for Literacy in History/Social Studies, Science, and Technical Subjects · Grades 11–12

Verbatim text of the standard

"Gather relevant information from multiple authoritative print and digital sources, using advanced searches effectively; assess the strengths and limitations of each source in terms of the specific task, purpose, and audience; integrate information into the text selectively to maintain the flow of ideas, avoiding plagiarism and overreliance on any one source and following a standard format for citation."

What students should be able to demonstrate
  • Locate authoritative print and digital sources within a discipline (history, science, or technical subject) using advanced search strategies
  • Judge each source's strengths and limitations against the specific task, purpose, and audience of the discipline
  • Integrate evidence selectively into discipline-specific writing without disrupting the flow of ideas
  • Avoid plagiarism and over-reliance on any single source; cite using a standard format

CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.W.11-12.8

Writing Standards · Research to Build and Present Knowledge · Grades 11–12

Verbatim text of the standard

"Gather relevant information from multiple authoritative print and digital sources, using advanced searches effectively; assess the strengths and limitations of each source in terms of the task, purpose, and audience; integrate information into the text selectively to maintain the flow of ideas, avoiding plagiarism and overreliance on any one source and following a standard format for citation."

What students should be able to demonstrate
  • Use advanced search techniques to locate authoritative sources for a research question in the ELA classroom
  • Assess each source's strengths and limitations relative to the task, purpose, and audience of the writing
  • Integrate information from multiple sources selectively, maintaining the flow of ideas in their own writing
  • Avoid plagiarism and over-reliance on any single source; cite using a standard format

Three items target the general-writing standard. Average performance is similar to WHST.8, but the spread is troubling: the easiest item on the test (Item 8, 73%) is here, alongside two underperforming "Moderate" items (#6 and #10) — students aren't transferring source-evaluation skills consistently across writing contexts.

What the item analysis tells us

Two patterns emerge from looking inside the test.

  1. Item 9 demands urgent attentionA 5% full-credit rate on any item is a flag. Whatever skill this item assesses, the entire student body — across all subgroups — failed to demonstrate it.
  2. Two "Moderate" items underperformedItems 6 and 10 (both W.8) scored 34% and 39% — well below the typical Moderate range. The pattern points to a specific gap in gathering and assessing source strengths, distinct from integrating sources into writing.

What the data reveals
The single largest performance gap on this IAB belongs to active English Learners (n=95, avg 2475) — sitting 100+ points below most other subgroups. Yet Reclassified Fluent EP students (n=300, avg 2623) outperform their English-Only peers. The pathway out of EL status is working; the support during EL status is where instructional design needs to focus.
